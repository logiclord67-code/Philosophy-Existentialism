# **The Trolley Problem: The Ethics of Sacrifice**

</div>

---

> “Is it better to let five people die through inaction, or kill one person through deliberate action?”

---

## **The Scenario: The Heavy Choice**

A runaway trolley is barreling down a track. Tied to the track ahead are **five people**. You are standing next to a lever. 

* **If you do nothing:** The trolley kills the five people.
* **If you pull the lever:** The trolley switches tracks and kills **one person** who is tied to the side track.

**What do you do?**

---

## **The War of Philosophies**

This problem highlights the clash between two major ways of thinking about right and wrong:

| The Utilitarian View | The Deontological View |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Logic:** "Save the many." | **Logic:** "Do no harm." |
| **Action:** Pull the lever. | **Action:** Do not pull the lever. |
| **Why:** The best outcome is the one that minimizes total suffering (5 lives > 1 life). | **Why:** Killing is inherently wrong. By pulling the lever, you become a murderer. |

---

## **3 Variations That Change Everything**

Philosophers use "tweaks" to this problem to see where your moral compass actually breaks.

### **1. The Fat Man (The Bridge)**
Same trolley, same five people. This time, you are on a bridge above the tracks. There is no lever. The only way to stop the trolley is to push a very large man off the bridge onto the tracks. 
* **The twist:** Most people who pull the lever in the first version refuse to push the man in this version. 
* **The question:** Is there a moral difference between *switching a track* and *physically pushing* someone to their death if the result (5 saved, 1 dead) is identical?

---

### **2. The Loophole**
The side track loops back to the main track. If you pull the lever, the one person on the side track is so large that their body will stop the trolley before it hits the five.
* **The question:** Are you using that person as a "tool" or a "means to an end"? Does your *intention* matter more than the *outcome*?

---

### **3. The Relation**
What if the one person on the side track is your mother, your child, or your best friend? 
* **The question:** Is "objective" math (5 > 1) still valid when personal love is involved? Should morality be "blind," or is it human to be biased?

---

## **The Modern 1‑Sentence Summary**

**The Trolley Problem reveals that our morals are rarely consistent—we want to save the most lives, but we are terrified of the guilt that comes from being the one who decided who dies.**

**One final question for you today:** *If the one person on the side track was a brilliant scientist about to cure cancer, and the five on the main track were convicted murderers, would your choice change?*

Try to find the "line" where your logic flips.
